AI Inside DIMS

This is where it all comes together. The AI chapter (05) taught you how detection works in general; here we follow a real YOLO detection through DIMS — from a video frame, to a box around a fire, to a single GPS coordinate on the map. This pipeline is the heart of the product.

If you read only one technical chapter closely, make it this one — it ties together AI (ch 05), maps & raycasting (ch 06), and the backend (ch 13).

The job: turn video into located alerts

A live video feed by itself is just pixels. DIMS’s AI layer has to answer two questions about every frame, fast enough to keep up with a flying drone:

  1. Is there something we care about? (fire, smoke, a crack, a defect) — that’s inference with a YOLO model.
  2. Where in the real world is it? — that’s the raycast step, unique to DIMS.

Step 1 — Detection (the YOLO models)

DIMS ships 7 production YOLO models as ONNX files — for example a fire_smoke model for the forest-fire use-case, plus models for structural defects and other inspection targets. The console even has a model selector so an operator can switch which model runs on the live feed.

Each model returns bounding boxes with a confidence score (e.g. fire 0.74). Some are segmentation models that also return a pixel-level polygon mask of the flames, not just a rectangle.

🔧 Fine-tuning

“Fine-tuning” means taking a general model and training it a little more on our data (real fire/smoke imagery) so it performs better on our exact problem. DIMS has a fine-tune pipeline for exactly this. A fine-tuned model is what produced the detection above — it boxes the plume far more confidently than a generic model.

Running it fast — the GPU

A flying drone produces many frames per second, so inference has to be quick. DIMS runs its models on an NVIDIA RTX 5080 GPU via CUDA (using onnxruntime-gpu). The speed-up is dramatic:

⚠ A real gotcha you’ll hit

The GPU build is fragile: a plain uv run can quietly revert the Python environment back to the CPU-only package, silently dropping you from 78 fps to 12. There’s a repair script (gpu-setup.ps1) and the rule “use --no-sync”. If inference suddenly gets slow, suspect this first.

Step 2 — Raycast (pixels → coordinates)

This is the step that makes DIMS an inspection system and not just a camera. A detection is a box in a 2D image; we need a real-world point. The coordinatizer service does this by raycasting (ch 06): it takes the drone’s exact pose and shoots a ray from the camera, through the centre of the detection box, until it hits a 3D model of the ground.

What it needs

  • The drone’s pose — position + the gimbal angles at the moment of capture. DIMS gets this from the drone’s OSD telemetry and from the XMP metadata embedded in each photo.
  • A surface to hit — a DSM (a GeoTIFF height map, read with rasterio) for terrain, or a 3D mesh (OBJ/PLY, read with trimesh) for structures. These come from DJI Terra (ch 11).
  • Correct coordinate maths — the surface is in a local projected system (UTM); the answer must be in global WGS84 lat/long. DIMS converts between them with pyproj and even corrects for meridian convergence. It also uses the M4TD’s per-camera lens parameters (intrinsics).
🔑 The 2D → 3D leap

A fire seen as a cluster of orange pixels becomes 36.32°N, 127.39°E. That single conversion is DIMS’s signature capability — it’s why an operator gets a pin on a map instead of “somewhere in this video”.

Want the deep version? Laser vs raycast for coordinates

There are two ways to geolocate a target. The primary method is the raycast described above — it works from any detection, using the terrain twin. The bonus method is the M4TD’s laser rangefinder (ch 08): point the laser at the target and it returns an exact distance, which combined with the drone pose gives a coordinate directly — no terrain model needed. DJI documents the laser “PinPoint” command for some older payloads but not officially for the M4TD, so DIMS treats raycast as primary and the laser as something to verify on real hardware.

Step 3 — Alert (one clean, located event)

The final step classifies a located detection into a severity, saves it as an alerts record, and fans it out — to the operator’s map, to mobile, and (for the forest-fire project) to the city’s disaster-safety platform — all within a ≤8-second target. Crucially, DIMS de-duplicates: the same fire seen across many frames must produce one alert, not hundreds, so detections within ~120 m and ~120 s of an existing alert are merged.

How accurate is it?

Honesty matters here. In the synthetic demo, the located coordinate lands roughly 24–74 m from the true fire — but most of that error is a datum gap between the terrain used to render the demo and the DSM used to raycast. The raycasting algorithm itself is accurate to about 1.4 m when measured directly on the DSM. On real missions, where the same DSM is used throughout, that rendering gap disappears.

For context, the project’s formal accuracy goals (the TIPS KPIs) include defect-detection F1 ≥ 98.5% and high-rate 3D defect mapping.
