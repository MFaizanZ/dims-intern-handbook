AI Inside DIMS

This is where it all comes together. The AI chapter (05) taught you how detection works in general; here we follow a real YOLO detection through DIMS — from a video frame, to a box around a fire, to a single GPS coordinate on the map. This pipeline is the heart of the product.

If you read only one technical chapter closely, make it this one — it ties together AI (ch 05), maps & raycasting (ch 06), and the backend (ch 13).

The job: turn video into located alerts

A live video feed by itself is just pixels. DIMS’s AI layer has to answer two questions about every frame, fast enough to keep up with a flying drone:

  1. Is there something we care about? (fire, smoke, a crack, a defect) — that’s inference with a YOLO model.
  2. Where in the real world is it? — that’s the raycast step, unique to DIMS.

Step 1 — Detection (the YOLO models)

DIMS ships 7 production YOLO models as ONNX files — for example a fire_smoke model for the forest-fire use-case, plus models for structural defects and other inspection targets. The console even has a model selector so an operator can switch which model runs on the live feed.

Each model returns bounding boxes with a confidence score (e.g. fire 0.74). Some are segmentation models that also return a pixel-level polygon mask of the flames, not just a rectangle.

🔧 Fine-tuning

“Fine-tuning” means taking a general model and training it a little more on our data (real fire/smoke imagery) so it performs better on our exact problem. DIMS has a fine-tune pipeline for exactly this. A fine-tuned model is what produced the detection above — it boxes the plume far more confidently than a generic model.

Step 2 — Raycast (pixels → coordinates)

This is the step that makes DIMS an inspection system and not just a camera. A detection is a box in a 2D image; we need a real-world point. The coordinatizer service does this by raycasting (ch 06): it takes the drone’s exact pose and shoots a ray from the camera, through the centre of the detection box, until it hits a 3D model of the ground.

What it needs

  • The drone’s pose — position + the gimbal angles at the moment of capture. DIMS gets this from the drone’s OSD telemetry and from the XMP metadata embedded in each photo.
  • A surface to hit — a DSM (a GeoTIFF height map, read with rasterio) for terrain, or a 3D mesh (OBJ/PLY, read with trimesh) for structures. These come from DJI Terra (ch 11).
  • Correct coordinate maths — the surface is in a local projected system (UTM); the answer must be in global WGS84 lat/long. DIMS converts between them with pyproj and even corrects for meridian convergence. It also uses the M4TD’s per-camera lens parameters (intrinsics).
🔑 The 2D → 3D leap

A fire seen as a cluster of orange pixels becomes 36.32°N, 127.39°E. That single conversion is DIMS’s signature capability — it’s why an operator gets a pin on a map instead of “somewhere in this video”.

The math — how a pixel becomes a coordinate

Here is the algorithm in full. It is classic ray casting: build one straight line (a “ray”) starting at the camera and pointing through the detected pixel, then find where that line first hits the ground. Everything below is just turning “a pixel + where the camera was + which way it looked” into a 3D line, and intersecting it with the terrain.

The algorithm, step by step

1. Pixel → a direction in the camera’s own frame. Normalise the pixel to the lens’s field of view (W·H = image size; HFOV/VFOV = the lens angles):

ax = (2·u / W − 1) · tan(HFOV / 2)
ay = (1 − 2·v / H) · tan(VFOV / 2)
d_cam = normalize( [ ax , ay , 1 ] )        # unit ray, +z = straight ahead

(Equivalently, with focal lengths/principal point: d_cam = normalize([ (u−cx)/fx , (v−cy)/fy , 1 ]).)

2. Rotate that direction into the real world. Build a rotation matrix R from the heading + gimbal angles, then rotate:

R = Rz(heading) · Ry(pitch) · Rx(roll)      # 3×3 rotation
d_world = R · d_cam                          # ray direction in world axes

(Getting pitch/roll/yaw from the gimbal right is the fiddly part — a parsing bug here is why DIMS specifically made sure gimbal_yaw/roll are read correctly.)

3. Place the ray’s origin. Convert the drone’s RTK fix into the same flat, metres-based frame as the surface (a local UTM / ENU frame):

C = geodetic_to_local( lat, lon, alt )       # cm-accurate origin, in metres

4. Shoot the ray and find the first hit. The ray is every point P(t) for distance t > 0:

P(t) = C + t · d_world

# DSM (height-grid): march along t, comparing the ray's height to the terrain,
# until the ray drops below the surface, then bisection-refine for the exact crossing:
find smallest t*  such that   P_z(t*) = h( P_x(t*), P_y(t*) )

# Mesh (OBJ/PLY): test the ray against triangles (Möller–Trumbore), keep the nearest hit.

5. Convert the hit back to GPS. P(t*) is in local metres; turn it back into global coordinates (correcting meridian convergence so true-north is exact):

(lat, lon, elev) = local_to_geodetic( P(t*) )    # pyproj: UTM → WGS84 (SRID 4326)

Output

A single real-world point — world_xyz = (lat, lon, elevation) — stored as a PostGIS PointZ in WGS84. That point is the alert’s location. One pixel in, one coordinate out.

📐 Why small angles matter

The ground error grows with distance: a pointing error of angle ε at slant range r lands roughly r · ε off. For example 0.5° (≈0.0087 rad) at 300 m ≈ 2.6 m on the ground. So RTK position (cm), gimbal-angle precision, lens calibration, and DSM resolution all feed the final accuracy — and the M4TD’s laser rangefinder (which measures r directly) is a great cross-check.

📐 For the mathematician — the named algorithms & full derivation

The pipeline is ray casting — a single primary ray, nearest-hit (the base case of ray tracing). It decomposes into four named sub-algorithms.

1 · Pixel → ray: inverse pinhole projection. With the intrinsic matrix K, back-project the homogeneous pixel x̃=[u,v,1]ᵀ:

K = | fx  0  cx |     fx = W / (2·tan(HFOV/2)),  fy = H / (2·tan(VFOV/2))
    |  0 fy  cy |     (cx,cy) = principal point ≈ image centre
    |  0  0   1 |

d_cam = K⁻¹ · x̃ ,   d̂_cam = d_cam / ‖d_cam‖     # unit ray in camera frame

2 · Orientation: a rotation in SO(3). Compose Tait–Bryan (Euler ZYX) angles from heading ψ, gimbal pitch θ, roll φ:

R = Rz(ψ)·Ry(θ)·Rx(φ)  ∈ SO(3)      (RᵀR = I,  det R = +1)
d = R · d̂_cam                        # ray direction in world axes
ray:  P(t) = C + t·d ,   t > 0       # C = camera origin (step 3)

3 · The intersection — this is the core algorithm, and it depends on the surface type.

Triangle mesh → the Möller–Trumbore algorithm (Möller & Trumbore, 1997). Solve C + t·d = (1−u−v)V₀ + u·V₁ + v·V₂ for the barycentric pair (u,v) and depth t via Cramer’s rule / triple products:

E1 = V1 − V0 ,  E2 = V2 − V0
P  = d × E2 ,   det = E1 · P              # |det| ≈ 0  ⇒  ray ∥ triangle (no hit)
inv = 1/det ,   T = C − V0
u = inv·(T · P)                           # valid hit needs  0 ≤ u
Q = T × E1
v = inv·(d · Q)                           #                  0 ≤ v  and  u+v ≤ 1
t = inv·(E2 · Q)                          #                  t > 0
hit = C + t·d ,   barycentric = (1−u−v, u, v)

Testing every triangle is O(n); DIMS uses a BVH (bounding-volume hierarchy) so nearest-hit is ~O(log n).

DSM raster (height-grid h(x,y)) → grid traversal + root-finding. Reduce to a 1-D root problem:

f(t) = P_z(t) − h( P_x(t), P_y(t) )       # h bilinearly interpolated within each cell
goal: smallest t* > 0 with f(t*) = 0

March the ray cell-by-cell with the Amanatides–Woo fast voxel-traversal algorithm (1987) — an exact grid DDA that visits every crossed cell in order, none skipped. When f changes sign across a cell the root is bracketed, then refine with bisection (or regula-falsi / secant) to tolerance. This is the “primary” path DIMS uses, since the terrain is a DSM.

4 · Local metres ↔ global coordinates: map projection (PROJ). Geodetic→ECEF uses the prime-vertical radius N(φ); in practice DIMS projects to UTM (Transverse Mercator) for a flat metric working frame and inverts at the end:

N(φ) = a / √(1 − e²·sin²φ)
X = (N+h)cosφ cosλ ,  Y = (N+h)cosφ sinλ ,  Z = (N(1−e²)+h) sinφ
… project/​invert via Transverse Mercator (pyproj/PROJ) …
meridian convergence  γ ≈ (λ − λ₀)·sinφ      # grid-north vs true-north, corrected so ψ is true

Error propagation & conditioning (first order): δP* ≈ J·δ(inputs) with Jacobian J = ∂P*/∂(C,R,K,surface). The dominant term turns an angular error δα into a ground error

‖δP*‖  ≈  r · δα / cos(i)
   r = slant range,   i = incidence angle between the ray and the surface normal

So the problem is ill-conditioned at grazing incidence (i → 90°, ray nearly tangent to a distant hillside): 1/cos(i) blows up. Steep, close shots — DIMS’s deliberate “close loiter” over the target — are the best-conditioned, which is exactly why that demo tactic tightened the error. This conditioning argument, not just the RTK spec, is the real reason the geometry is chosen the way it is.

Want the deep version? Laser vs raycast for coordinates

There are two ways to geolocate a target. The primary method is the raycast described above — it works from any detection, using the terrain twin. The bonus method is the M4TD’s laser rangefinder (ch 08): point the laser at the target and it returns an exact distance, which combined with the drone pose gives a coordinate directly — no terrain model needed. DJI documents the laser “PinPoint” command for some older payloads but not officially for the M4TD, so DIMS treats raycast as primary and the laser as something to verify on real hardware.

Step 3 — Alert (one clean, located event)

The final step classifies a located detection into a severity, saves it as an alerts record, and fans it out — to the operator’s map, to mobile, and (for the forest-fire project) to the city’s disaster-safety platform — all within a ≤8-second target. Crucially, DIMS de-duplicates: the same fire seen across many frames must produce one alert, not hundreds, so detections within ~120 m and ~120 s of an existing alert are merged.

How accurate is it?

Honesty matters here. In the synthetic demo, the located coordinate lands roughly 24–74 m from the true fire — but most of that error is a datum gap between the terrain used to render the demo and the DSM used to raycast. The raycasting algorithm itself is accurate to about 1.4 m when measured directly on the DSM. On real missions, where the same DSM is used throughout, that rendering gap disappears.

For context, the project’s formal accuracy goals (the TIPS KPIs) include defect-detection F1 ≥ 98.5% and high-rate 3D defect mapping.
