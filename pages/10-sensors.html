Sensors & Payloads

A drone is only as smart as the camera bolted under it. This chapter walks through every kind of sensor a DIMS-relevant drone can carry — in plain language — and matches each one to the inspection jobs it does best.

First: what is a “payload”?

The payload is the equipment a drone carries to do its actual job — almost always a camera or sensor package. It hangs underneath the aircraft on a gimbal: a motorised mount that keeps the camera steady and lets it point in any direction, no matter how the drone tilts or shakes. Think of the gimbal as a self-balancing wrist and the payload as the eye it holds.

Different jobs need different sensors. A crack inspection needs a sharp zoom camera; a fire patrol needs to see heat; a 3D model needs to measure shape. Below are the seven payload types that matter for DIMS, each with what it is, how it works, and what it’s good for.

1. RGB / visible cameras

What it is. A normal colour camera — “RGB” just means red-green-blue, the light your own eyes see. This is the bread-and-butter sensor on every drone.

How it works. Light hits a sensor chip and becomes a high-resolution photo. The number that matters is megapixels (millions of dots) — more megapixels means you can zoom into the picture and still see fine detail. Inspection drones usually carry several RGB lenses at once:

  • Wide — sees the whole scene, good for situational awareness and 3D mapping.
  • Tele / zoom — magnifies a far-away target. This is the inspector’s favourite: a zoom lens lets the drone spot a hairline crack from tens of metres away, so it never has to fly dangerously close to a structure.

One technical word you’ll meet: mechanical vs electronic (rolling) shutter — a mechanical shutter captures the whole frame in one instant (no smear on fast-moving or mapping shots), while a rolling shutter reads the image line-by-line and can slightly skew fast motion.

🧩 Analogy

Zoom is the drone’s pair of binoculars. Instead of walking up to a tower crane bolt — risky and slow — it stands back at a safe distance and reads the bolt through high-power glass.

Why zoom matters for DIMS

For the tower-crane inspection project, a strong zoom RGB lens is the primary tool — it’s what lets the drone read tiny defects on steel from a safe standoff distance.

2. Thermal / infrared (LWIR)

What it is. A camera that sees heat instead of light. The proper name is LWIR (long-wave infrared) — a thermal camera.

How it works. Everything warmer than its surroundings emits infrared energy. A thermal sensor reads that emitted heat directly, so it works in total darkness, through smoke, and at night — it doesn’t need any visible light at all. A radiometric thermal camera goes further and reads an actual temperature for every pixel, not just “hot vs cold”.

🧩 Analogy

Thermal is a pair of heat-vision goggles. A person hiding in the dark, an overheating fuse box, or a smouldering hotspot before it bursts into flame — all glow brightly even when an ordinary camera sees nothing.

What it’s good for. Fire and smoke detection (the Bomunsan forest-fire job leans on this), spotting overheating electrical gear before it fails, finding water ingress (wet patches read cooler), and measuring building heat loss.

3. Laser rangefinder

What it is. A small laser that measures the exact distance from the drone to whatever it’s pointed at.

How it works. It fires a laser pulse, times how long the reflection takes to come back, and converts that to a precise distance. The Matrice 4TD carries one with a range up to roughly 1,800 m.

Why it’s a big deal for DIMS. Distance alone is useful, but combine it with three things the drone already knows — its own GPS position, its altitude, and the exact angle its gimbal is pointing — and you can compute the real-world coordinate of the target directly. That is geolocation by laser: point at a fire, get its latitude/longitude.

🔑 Connect it back

This is the hardware cousin of raycasting — the software trick DIMS uses to turn a pixel in the video into a map pin (see chapter 06 and chapter 15). The laser gives a direct measured distance; raycasting estimates it from a 3D terrain model. Either way the goal is the same: pixels → a pin on the map.

4. LiDAR

What it is. Like a rangefinder, but firing hundreds of thousands of laser pulses per second in a sweeping pattern to build a dense 3D map of a whole structure. DJI’s versions are the Zenmuse L-series.

How it works. Each pulse measures one distance; millions of them together form a point cloud — a 3D constellation of dots that traces the exact shape of everything the drone flew over.

What it’s good for. Building accurate 3D models, measuring volume and structure (e.g. a stockpile or a building), and — usefully — seeing through vegetation: some pulses slip between leaves and hit the ground, so LiDAR can map terrain hidden under a forest canopy.

5. Multispectral

What it is. A camera that captures several specific, narrow bands of light at once — including bands just outside what your eye can see.

How it works. Instead of blending everything into one colour image, a multispectral sensor records each chosen band separately. Healthy plants, stressed plants, soil, and different building materials each reflect these bands differently, so comparing the bands reveals things a normal photo hides.

What it’s good for. Vegetation health (a classic in agriculture — spotting stressed crops before they look sick to the eye), and distinguishing materials by how they reflect.

6. SWIR (short-wave infrared)

What it is. A sensor that sees a band beyond visible light but before thermal — short-wave infrared, or SWIR.

How it works. SWIR reflects off surfaces (like visible light) rather than being emitted as heat (like thermal), but at wavelengths that interact strongly with water and chemical bonds. That makes it unusually good at revealing moisture, material composition, and hidden detail that an RGB camera simply cannot show.

7. How sensors combine — “sensor fusion”

No single sensor wins at everything. The real power comes from carrying several and fusing what they each see into one complete picture — this is called sensor fusion.

RGB answers “what does it look like?” Sharp colour detail — readable text, rust, hairline cracks, the exact shape of a defect. It’s the sensor a human inspector trusts most. But it’s blind in darkness and smoke, and a flat photo carries no distance.

Thermal answers “what’s hot?” It sees through smoke and night, and flags heat the eye can’t — a fire starting, a fuse overheating, damp seeping into a wall. But it’s low-resolution and can’t read fine text or colour.

LiDAR (and the laser) answer “what shape, how far?” They turn a scene into measured 3D geometry and exact distances — the backbone of a digital twin and of geolocating a target. But a point cloud alone has no colour and no temperature.

Put together: RGB for detail, thermal for heat, LiDAR for shape, the laser for range. Fused, they describe a scene more completely than any one could — exactly what DIMS needs to detect a problem, identify it, and pin its location. Want the deep version? Why “fusion” is harder than just bolting on cameras

To fuse two sensors you have to know exactly where each one was pointing at the exact same instant — their viewpoints must be aligned (“registered”) so a hot pixel in the thermal frame lines up with the right crack in the RGB frame and the right point in the LiDAR cloud. That requires precise, time-synchronised gimbal angles and positions. It’s the same maths that powers geolocation: get the angles and position right, and every sensor’s view snaps onto the same 3D world. Get them wrong by a degree and your fire pin lands on the wrong hillside.
