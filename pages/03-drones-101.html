Drones & Robotics 101

Before we can talk about autonomous inspection, you need to know what a drone actually is and how it flies. This chapter starts from absolute zero — no prior drone knowledge assumed — and gives you the vocabulary every other chapter relies on.

Drone vs. the whole system: UAV and UAS

When people say “drone” they usually mean the flying machine itself. The precise term for that aircraft is a UAV — an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle, i.e. an aircraft with no pilot on board. But the aircraft never works alone. The complete package — the aircraft plus the controller, the ground station, the dock, and the software that commands it — is called a UAS, an Unmanned Aerial System.

🧩 Analogy

A “smartphone” is the handset, but the thing that makes it useful is the whole system: the phone, the cell towers, the carrier’s network, and the apps. A UAV is the handset; a UAS is the network behind it. DIMS is the “app + network” layer of a UAS — the drone is just one part it commands.

Multirotor vs. fixed-wing

Drones come in two broad families. A fixed-wing drone looks like a small aeroplane: it needs forward speed to stay up and can’t stop in mid-air — great for covering huge distances, useless for hovering next to a crack on a bridge. A multirotor drone (several spinning propellers) can take off straight up, hover perfectly still, and creep sideways centimetre by centimetre.

Inspection is all about parking a camera in exactly the right spot and holding it there. That is why every drone CNS uses — the DJI Matrice class — is a multirotor.

How a quadcopter actually flies

The most common multirotor is the quadcopter: four motors, each spinning one propeller. A spinning propeller pushes air downward, and the air pushes back up — that upward push is lift. Spin all four faster and the drone rises; slow them all and it sinks. Match lift exactly to the drone’s weight and it hovers.

The clever part is steering. The drone never tilts a propeller — it just changes the relative speeds of the four motors:

  • Up / down (throttle): all four speed up or slow down together.
  • Pitch (tilt forward/back): the rear pair spins faster than the front pair, so the drone noses down and slides forward.
  • Roll (tilt left/right): one side spins faster than the other, sliding the drone sideways.
  • Yaw (spin to face a new direction): the two diagonal pairs spin in opposite directions; speeding up one pair twists the whole aircraft.
🧩 Analogy

Think of four people lifting a tabletop, one at each corner. Everyone lifts equally → the table rises flat (hover). The two back people lift harder → the table tips forward and slides that way (pitch). That tiny, constant rebalancing — hundreds of times per second — is exactly what the drone’s “brain” does to fly.

The key parts of a drone

You don’t need to be an engineer, but you should recognise the main components when they come up:

PartWhat it does (in plain English)
Flight controllerThe “brain.” A small computer that runs the balancing maths and converts your commands (“go forward”) into motor speeds.
IMUThe “inner ear.” An accelerometer + gyroscope that senses tilt and motion, telling the brain which way is up and how fast it’s turning.
GNSS receiverThe satellite-positioning chip (GPS is one example) that tells the drone where it is on Earth.
ESCsElectronic Speed Controllers — the throttles that translate the brain’s orders into precise motor power.
Motors + propellersThe muscles. They spin the props that make lift.
BatteryThe fuel tank. Flight time (often tens of minutes) is set by how much energy it holds.
Radio / transmission linkThe wireless connection between the drone and its controller (or dock) carrying commands up and video + telemetry down.

Positioning — and why centimetres matter

Your phone’s GPS is accurate to several metres. That’s fine for “turn left at the next street,” but it’s a disaster for inspection: if the drone thinks it’s 3 metres away from where it really is, it can’t reliably photograph the same bolt on the same crane on every flight, and the coordinate it reports for a defect would be off by a car-length.

The fix is RTK — Real-Time Kinematic positioning. A second, fixed receiver (a base station) sits at a precisely known spot on the ground and measures the exact same satellite errors the drone is seeing. It broadcasts a correction, and the drone subtracts that error in real time — sharpening its position from several metres down to roughly one centimetre.

🔑 The one idea to remember

Plain GNSS = “somewhere on this block.” RTK = “this exact point.” That centimetre-level precision is what lets DIMS turn a defect in a video into a trustworthy map coordinate, and lets the drone repeat the identical route every single time.

The camera setup: gimbal + payload

A drone that hovers perfectly still is no use if its camera is shaking. So the camera is mounted on a gimbal — a motorised cradle that uses its own little motors and sensors to keep the camera rock-steady and pointed where you aim it, even as the drone tilts and bobs in the wind. The gimbal can also steer the camera independently: tilt down at the ground, swing to track a target.

What the gimbal carries is called the payload — the actual sensors. An inspection payload typically bundles a normal RGB (colour) camera, a zoom camera for distant detail, and a thermal camera that sees heat (vital for spotting fire or overheating equipment). We cover every sensor type in chapter 10.

Levels of autonomy

“Autonomous” isn’t all-or-nothing — it’s a ladder. Climbing it is the whole story of how DIMS removes the human from the field:

  1. Manual flying. A pilot stands on site with a controller, watching the drone and steering every move. Maximum control, maximum human effort.
  2. Waypoint / wayline missions. You pre-define a route — a wayline of GPS waypoints with altitudes and camera actions — and the drone flies it on its own while a pilot supervises nearby.
  3. Fully autonomous dock missions. The drone lives in a dock in the field and flies scheduled missions with no human present at all — often BVLOS (Beyond Visual Line Of Sight), meaning no one can even see the aircraft. This is the level DIMS targets.
🧩 The “drone-in-a-box”

The DJI Dock 3 is a weatherproof box that charges, shelters, and launches the drone by itself — think of a robotic garage that opens its roof, sends the car out on its route, then brings it home and recharges it, with nobody around. That self-sufficiency is what makes truly hands-off inspection possible. We dissect the dock in chapter 07.

Safety basics every operator must know

A drone is a fast-spinning machine in the air, so a handful of safety systems exist on every serious platform. You’ll meet them in depth later; learn the names now:

  • RTH (Return-To-Home): a one-button — or automatic — command that flies the drone back to its launch/dock point. It triggers on its own if the battery runs low or the radio link drops.
  • Geofence: an invisible 3D fence. You define the area (and height) the drone is allowed in, and the firmware refuses to cross it — keeping it away from airports, people, or no-fly zones.
  • Failsafes: automatic “if something goes wrong, do the safe thing” rules — lost signal → RTH, critical battery → land now, sensor fault → hold position.
  • AGL vs. sea-level altitude: “100 m altitude” is ambiguous. AGL (Above Ground Level) is height over the terrain right below the drone; sea-level (ASL) is height above the ocean. On a hill like Bomunsan the ground rises and falls, so a route that’s safe in sea-level numbers can still fly the drone into a slope. Terrain clearance always means AGL.
⚠ Why this matters for DIMS

Because DIMS can fly missions with no one watching, these safety systems aren’t optional extras — they’re the only thing standing between “a routine inspection” and “a crash.” Several later chapters are devoted entirely to them.
